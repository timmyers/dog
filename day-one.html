Supplies Checklist

Check off what you already have from inherited stuff. Items marked may have are things commonly passed along.

Sleeping & Crate

  • 42" wire crate with divider panel MidWest iCrate or Life Stages. Divider lets you size up as she grows toward 40-55 lbs.
  • Crate pad/mat Chew-resistant, flat — avoid loose stuffing for now
  • Snuggle Puppy heartbeat toy Helps first few nights away from littermates
  • Light blanket or old t-shirt Familiar scent from foster if possible
  • Exercise pen (36" tall) Safe contained area when you can't supervise directly

Feeding

  • Stainless steel bowls (2) Non-tip, non-slip. One food, one water. Not plastic — harbors bacteria.
  • Puppy food — rescue's current brand Ask what she's been eating — you'll transition gradually over 10-14 days
  • Puppy food — your chosen brand See feeding plan below for recommendations
  • Airtight food storage container
  • Collapsible travel water bowl For hikes once vaccinations complete

Training

  • 6-foot leash (nylon or biothane) Not retractable
  • Flat buckle collar (small/medium) Attach ID tag immediately
  • ID tag Your name, phone, address. Same-day at PetSmart/Petco.
  • Front-clip harness Ruffwear Front Range or Blue-9 Balance, size Small. Better than collar for puppy walks.
  • Long line (15-30 ft) For recall practice. Biothane is lightweight and easy to clean.
  • Training treats Zuke's Mini Naturals, boiled chicken, or string cheese. Keep to 10% of daily calories.
  • Treat pouch Clips to waist for easy access
  • Clicker (optional) Karen Pryor i-Click
  • Enzymatic cleaner ESSENTIAL. Nature's Miracle or Rocco & Roxie. Regular cleaners don't remove scent to a dog's nose.
  • Puppy pads Emergency indoor use, nighttime, bad weather

Safety & Puppy-Proofing

  • Baby gates (2-3) Block stairs and off-limits rooms
  • Bitter apple spray Grannick's. For furniture, cords, baseboards.
  • Cord covers Electrical cords are a serious chewing hazard
  • Secure trash cans Locking lids or behind closed doors
  • Remove toxic plants Aloe vera, ivy, lilies, pothos, sago palm

Grooming

  • Slicker brush Aussie double coat needs brushing 3-4x/week. Hertzko Self-Cleaning or Chris Christensen.
  • Steel comb For tangles behind ears and chest
  • Puppy shampoo Burt's Bees or Earthbath. Bathe once a month max.
  • Nail clippers or grinder Start handling paws early to desensitize
  • Styptic powder (Kwik Stop) In case you nick the quick
  • Ear cleaning solution Virbac Epi-Otic
  • Dog toothbrush + enzymatic toothpaste Never human toothpaste. Start dental habits early.

Outdoor & Walking

  • Poop bags + dispenser Highlands Ranch has strict pet waste ordinances on trails
  • Reflective collar clip or light For evening walks
  • Puppy jacket March mornings in HR are still cold at 5,900 ft. Ruffwear or Kurgo.

Health — First Week

  • Vet visit within 2-3 days Highlands Ranch Animal Clinic (38 W Springer Dr, AAHA-certified) or Inspire Animal Hospital
  • Vaccination records from RezDawg
  • Confirm microchip + update registration to your info
  • Pet insurance Enroll day one — Trupanion, Healthy Paws, or Lemonade. Most have waiting periods.
  • Flea/tick/heartworm prevention Vet will recommend. CO has ticks on foothill trails. Simparica Trio covers fleas, ticks, heartworm in one chew.
  • MDR1 gene test Ask vet — herding breeds can be sensitive to common medications

Training Treats

Keep treats to 10% of daily calories. Best options:

  • Zuke's Mini Naturals — ~3 cal each, soft, easy to break smaller
  • Plain boiled chicken breast — pea-sized pieces, high value, no additives
  • String cheese — small pieces, high-value for tough training moments
  • Baby carrots or blueberries — low-calorie, good for teething
  • Pupford Freeze-Dried — 1 cal each, single ingredient

Water

  • Fresh water available at all times during the day
  • Guideline: 1/2 to 1 oz per pound of body weight per day
  • Highlands Ranch is dry at 5,900 ft — she may drink more than average
  • Pick up water bowl 2-3 hours before bedtime to reduce overnight accidents
  • After play, let her drink but don't allow gulping large amounts at once

Toxic Foods — Never Feed

Most dangerous (can be fatal):
  • Xylitol — in sugar-free gum, candy, some peanut butters. Always check labels.
  • Chocolate — dark/baker's worst. Causes seizures, heart failure.
  • Grapes & raisins — acute kidney failure, even small amounts.
  • Onions, garlic, leeks, chives — damage red blood cells. Cooked or raw.
  • Macadamia nuts — weakness, tremors, hyperthermia.
  • Alcohol — even small amounts dangerous.
Also dangerous: cooked bones (splinter), avocado, caffeine, yeast dough, fatty trimmings/bacon grease (pancreatitis), corn cobs (blockage).

Day One Schedule

Arrival
Car ride home: Keep calm. Bring towel, paper towels, water, treats. Crate or carrier in the car is safest. She may get carsick.
First stop: Go DIRECTLY to the designated potty spot in the yard. Stand quietly, let her sniff. Praise and treat immediately when she goes.
First 30 min: Let her explore ONE room (main living area). Keep it low-key. No overwhelming introductions.
Water & food: Show her the water bowl. Offer a small meal if it's been a few hours. Don't worry if she won't eat — stress suppresses appetite.
Morning — Settling In
Every 30 min: Take her outside to the SAME potty spot. Praise/treat for success. No punishment for accidents.
Crate intro: Place treats inside the open crate. Let her walk in and out freely. Feed a small meal inside with the door open. Don't close the door yet.
Short play: 10-15 minutes of gentle play, then enforced rest. Puppies need 18-20 hours of sleep per day. Overtired puppies get nippy and wild.
Midday
Meal: Feed in or near the crate.
Potty: Immediately after eating — puppies almost always need to go within 5-15 min of a meal.
Nap: Encourage a nap in the crate. Briefly close the door while you sit nearby. Open before she gets distressed. Build duration over days, not hours.
After nap: Straight outside to potty, then gentle play or exploration.
Afternoon
Potty breaks: Every 30-45 min. After every nap, meal, and play session. Watch for circling or floor-sniffing.
Exploration: Let her sniff a second room. Keep it calm.
Handling practice: Gently touch paws, ears, mouth, tail. Pair with tiny treats. Builds tolerance for grooming and vet visits.
Afternoon meal: In or near the crate. Potty break immediately after.
Evening
Evening meal: Keep the schedule consistent.
Calm time: Let her be near you. Chew toys are great. No rough play — you want a calm puppy by bedtime.
7-8 PM: Pick up water bowl (2-3 hours before bedtime).
Last potty: Right before crate time. Wait outside until she goes, even if it takes a while.
Bedtime & Overnight
9-10 PM: Place her in the crate in your bedroom. Snuggle Puppy toy helps. Cover crate partially with a blanket.
Crying: Some whining is normal. If she settles within 10-15 min, let her be. If intense, sit by the crate — wait for a pause in crying before letting her out for a quick potty break.
2-3 AM: Set an alarm. She WILL need an overnight potty break (puppies can hold it ~1 hour per month of age). Carry her outside, let her go, straight back in the crate. No play, no excitement, lights dim.
5:30-6:30 AM: She'll wake early. Immediately carry her outside to potty. Then breakfast.

Day One Ground Rules

  1. No visitors. Let Ilia bond with you first. Other people and animals can be introduced gradually over the coming week.
  2. No dog parks or public floors. She's not fully vaccinated yet. Your yard is fine.
  3. Keep things quiet. No loud music, no roughhousing. She just left everything she knew.
  4. Supervise 100% when she's out of the crate or pen. If you can't watch her, she goes in.
  5. Accidents will happen. Enzymatic cleaner, no punishment. Ever.
  6. Start as you mean to go on. If she's not allowed on the couch as an adult, don't let her on it today.
Remember the 3-3-3 rule: 3 days to decompress, 3 weeks to settle in, 3 months to truly bond. Be patient — the real Ilia will emerge over time.
